Companies do not leave the Middle East easily. The region accounts for roughly $4 trillion in GDP across the Gulf Cooperation Council states alone, hosts some of the world's largest sovereign wealth funds, and sits at the center of global energy and logistics infrastructure. When conflict escalates, the question for most multinationals is not whether to exit — it is how to keep operating while absorbing costs and risks that did not exist 30 days ago.

Here is what that actually looks like on the ground.

## The Companies Still There, and Why

BP, Shell, and TotalEnergies maintain significant upstream operations in the Gulf despite sustained regional volatility. Saudi Aramco's joint venture partners — including ExxonMobil and Sinopec — are not walking away from projects tied to the world's largest proven oil reserves. Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud have each made multi-billion dollar infrastructure commitments to UAE and Saudi Arabia data center expansions that predate the current conflict cycle and remain on track as of Q1 2025.

The calculus is straightforward: conflict-adjacent stability in UAE and Saudi Arabia has held through multiple prior escalation cycles. The UAE has not experienced direct military conflict on its territory in modern history. Riyadh has become a regional hub for multinationals explicitly because of its political stability relative to the broader region. Companies are not operating in the Middle East despite the risk — they are operating in specific parts of it because the risk-adjusted returns still pencil out.

What changes in 30 days is not the fundamental investment thesis. It is the operating cost structure.

## What the Red Sea Disruption Has Done to Logistics

The most immediate and quantifiable impact for companies with Middle East supply chains is not in-country — it is on the water.

Houthi attacks on commercial vessels in the Red Sea, which intensified from late 2023 onward, forced a majority of major container shipping lines to reroute via the Cape of Good Hope. Maersk, MSC, and CMA CGM all suspended Red Sea transits for extended periods. The result: Asia-to-Europe transit times lengthened by 10 to 14 days, and spot freight rates on the Shanghai-Rotterdam lane increased by 173% between December 2023 and January 2024, according to the Drewry World Container Index.

For companies importing components from Asia through the Suez Canal — electronics manufacturers, automotive suppliers, fast-moving consumer goods distributors — this is not an abstraction. It is a direct hit to landed cost and inventory planning. A 14-day transit extension on a just-in-time supply chain does not just cost money. It requires safety stock increases, accelerated air freight decisions, and supplier communication that consumes operational bandwidth that is already stretched.

Freightos data from the same period showed air freight rates on Middle East-connected lanes running 4 to 6 times higher than equivalent sea freight per kilogram. Companies that had not pre-positioned air freight capacity contracts were paying spot premiums to maintain continuity.

## War-Risk Insurance: The Cost That Surprises Finance Teams

Before the current conflict cycle, war-risk insurance premiums for vessels transiting the Red Sea were approximately 0.05% of vessel value per voyage — essentially negligible. By early 2024, according to Lloyd's Market Association data, that figure had risen to between 0.5% and 1.0% per voyage, representing a 10x to 20x increase.

For a $100 million vessel, that is a per-voyage insurance cost that moved from $50,000 to between $500,000 and $1 million.

This cost does not stay on the shipping company's books. It flows through to freight rates, which flow through to landed costs for every importer and exporter using the route. Finance teams at companies with significant import exposure from Asia are now working through the math on how much of this cost increase is permanent (route length, fuel) versus recoverable (insurance premiums if threat levels decline).

On the cargo side, standard marine insurance policies under Institute Cargo Clauses (A) exclude war and strikes risks unless specifically endorsed. Many procurement and logistics teams discovered this exclusion gap only when filing claims on damaged or delayed shipments. Checking the war-risk endorsement status of all active marine policies is now a routine item on risk review agendas for any company with Middle East logistics exposure.

## Country by Country: The Risk Is Not Uniform

The phrase "Middle East conflict" obscures what is actually a highly differentiated risk landscape across individual countries.

**UAE**: Dubai and Abu Dhabi continue to function as regional business hubs with no direct conflict exposure. The UAE's diplomatic positioning — maintaining relations with both Israel and Arab states — has so far insulated it from becoming a direct conflict theater. DIFC registrations and new foreign business licenses have continued to grow through 2024. Risk rating: elevated monitoring, not operational disruption.

**Saudi Arabia**: Riyadh's Vision 2030 megaprojects are proceeding. NEOM construction continues. Foreign investment inflows hit a record $26.3 billion in 2022 and remained elevated through 2024 according to Saudi Arabian General Investment Authority data. The 2019 Aramco attacks demonstrated that Saudi Arabia can absorb significant infrastructure strikes and recover export capacity within days. Risk rating: supply chain adjacency, not direct operational impairment.

**Israel**: A fundamentally different situation. Companies with Israeli operations — technology firms, pharmaceutical manufacturers, defense contractors — are managing active conflict conditions. Intel's Kiryat Gat fab, Mobileye's Jerusalem engineering center, and dozens of cybersecurity and deep-tech companies are operating under security protocols that would have been unthinkable two years ago. Talent retention and recruitment have become material business risks. Several European multinationals have relocated regional management positions to Cyprus or Greece as interim measures.

**Jordan**: Largely stable and functioning as a logistics and humanitarian corridor. The Port of Aqaba has absorbed some Red Sea rerouting traffic. Risk rating: low direct exposure, elevated monitoring for regional spillover.

**Yemen and Gaza**: These are not business operating environments for international companies. They are conflict zones requiring complete operational withdrawal and employee evacuation.

## What "Business as Usual" Actually Means Right Now

For companies in the Gulf states, "business as usual" has a specific meaning: the meetings still happen, the projects still move, the contracts still close — but the risk management overhead has approximately tripled.

Security briefings that used to be quarterly are now weekly. Travel approval processes that were self-service now require regional security director sign-off. Insurance renewals that used to be administrative are now strategic negotiations. Supplier qualification processes now include conflict-exposure assessments as standard criteria.

The companies managing this best are those that built institutional knowledge from prior cycles — 2019, 2020, 2022. They have playbooks. They have pre-approved alternative suppliers. They have insurance riders already in place. They are not inventing the response in real time.

The companies struggling are those that entered the region during the post-COVID investment boom of 2021 to 2023, attracted by Gulf state diversification incentives, without building the operational infrastructure to manage a conflict cycle.

## The 30-Day, 90-Day, and Long-Term Picture

Thirty days in, the primary costs are logistics, insurance, and management bandwidth. Most companies in the Gulf states are absorbing these as elevated operating expenses, not existential threats.

At 90 days, the picture depends entirely on whether the conflict remains geographically contained. If it expands to involve the Strait of Hormuz — which handles 21% of global petroleum liquids according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration — the scenario changes from "elevated operating costs" to "fundamental supply chain architecture review."

Long term, the structural shifts already underway — Gulf state economic diversification, non-oil FDI growth, digital infrastructure investment — are not reversing. The region's medium-term investment case remains intact for companies with the risk tolerance and operational capacity to manage through conflict cycles.

The ones still operating there 30 days in understand that this is, for better or worse, what operating in the Middle East has always required.
